Pause and Reflect

What the Numbers Tell You Together

Four questions about the simple case you just worked through

In the interactive exercise, you stepped through eight transactions and watched the financial statements build themselves. Now step back and think about what you saw. Each prompt below repeats the relevant transaction and its effects, then asks a question. The questions are not about individual numbers — they are about what you can learn when you look at the numbers together.

Your responses are saved locally on your device and are not submitted anywhere.

Transaction: Service Revenue
The entity provided $43,000 of services. It collected $25,000 in cash immediately; the remaining $18,000 is owed by customers who will pay later.
Cash+$25,000
Accounts Receivable+$18,000
Retained Earnings+$43,000
Prompt 1 of 4
The system recorded $43,000 in revenue. The cash flow statement shows $25,000 collected. These are not competing numbers — they are two facts about the same event. What does comparing them tell you about this entity’s situation right now?
Transaction: Depreciation
The entity recorded $4,000 of depreciation on its equipment. The equipment is wearing out — its recorded worth decreases, and the cost is recognized as an expense. No cash moves.
Equipment($4,000)
Retained Earnings($4,000)
Cashno change
Prompt 2 of 4
No cash moved, but the income statement recorded a $4,000 expense and the balance sheet reduced the equipment’s recorded worth. What is the system trying to capture with this entry? What would be missing from the picture if the system only recorded events that involved cash?
After All Transactions
The entity completed its first year. Here are two numbers from the same set of transactions, decomposed differently:
Net Income (from income statement)$24,000
Cash from Operations (from cash flow statement)$22,000
Gap$2,000
The gap comes from two sources: $18,000 of revenue not yet collected in cash (offset by $12,000 collected from an earlier receivable), and $4,000 of depreciation expense that involved no cash.
Prompt 3 of 4
Net income and cash from operations come from the same set of transactions, decomposed differently. What does the gap between them tell you? What questions would it lead you to ask about this entity?
The Full Set of Statements
You now have three decompositions of the change between the opening and closing balance sheets: the statement of changes in equities (the full bridge), the cash flow statement (the change in cash), and the income statement (the operating detail of one line in the equities statement). Each one organizes the same underlying transactions differently.
Prompt 4 of 4
Different people come to these statements with different questions — a lender, an owner, a regulator. What can they learn from looking at the decompositions together that they could not learn from any single one?
