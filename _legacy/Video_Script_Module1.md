## OPENING — Who this is for

Hi. I'm Rick Antle. I teach accounting at Yale, and I designed this prework for students going into their first accounting course — MBA programs, law schools, policy programs, anywhere you'll encounter financial information and want to understand what it's telling you and what it isn't.

You might have no accounting background at all. That's fine — this prework assumes none. Or you might have taken an accounting course before. That's fine too — you'll find that we approach familiar material from a different angle. Either way, this is where we start.

---

## THE INFORMATION SYSTEMS FAMILY

Let me begin with a question that might seem odd: what does accounting have in common with a climate model?

You already rely on information systems, even if you don't call them that. A scale tells you how much something weighs. A tape measure tells you how long something is. Simple systems — they report on a single, well-defined physical property.

But many of the things we want to understand aren't like that. We want to understand them, and we make better choices when we do — but they don't reduce to a single property that an instrument can read.

Think about a climate model. It takes ocean temperatures, atmospheric chemistry, land use patterns, solar radiation data — and produces projections. But the projections depend on assumptions, and different assumptions produce different projections. A coastal city planner comes to that model wanting to know about sea levels. A farmer in the Midwest wants to know about rainfall patterns. An insurance company wants to know about hurricane frequency. Same underlying science, same data — but they come with different questions, and the questions shape what they look for in the output.

Or think about something closer to everyday business. A CEO's dashboard takes thousands of data points about a company and presents a handful of numbers. Which numbers? That's a design choice. The CFO wants to know if cash covers next month's payroll. The head of sales wants to know which product lines are growing. The board wants to know if the company hit its annual targets. Same organization, same underlying activity — different questions.

What do these systems have in common? They all exist because the thing they inform us about is too complex to observe directly. They all compress — taking vast, messy reality and producing something structured enough to work with. And because they compress, they make choices about what to include and what to leave out. Those choices involve tradeoffs. The output is useful, but it is never the whole picture. It always requires a person who understands what the system did and what it didn't.

Accounting systems belong to this family. They are information systems that evolved — over centuries — to help people make sense of the economic activity of organizations, something no individual can observe directly once an organization reaches any real scale. Like a climate model, they depend on assumptions and conventions. Like a dashboard, they compress, and the people who use them come with different questions. An investor, a lender, a manager, a tax authority, a judge — same organization, same underlying economic activity, different questions.

That is what this prework is about.

## ARCHITECTURE AND INSTANCE

Now, here's something that will organize everything you're about to learn.

Every accounting system on earth — every single one — shares the same deep structure. There's an identity at the center that forces everything to balance. There's a double-entry system that records every economic event in two places. There are four output channels — four financial statements — that present different views of the same underlying data. And there's a boundary that determines which economic events get into the system and which don't.

None of this was designed in one fell swoop. Double-entry bookkeeping emerged in medieval Italy. The identity crystallized through centuries of practice. The four-statement structure took shape over generations. What's remarkable is that these elements survived and spread — across cultures, across centuries — because they worked. They solved the complexity problem better than anything else people came up with.

We call that deep structure the architecture. It doesn't vary. A corporation in New York, a nonprofit in Nairobi, a government agency in Berlin — the architecture is the same.

What does vary is the specific set of rules that a particular rule-making body writes for a particular class of organizations. In the United States, the Financial Accounting Standards Board writes the rules for for-profit companies — that set of rules is called US GAAP. Internationally, the IASB writes a different set — that's called IFRS. For US state and local governments, there's yet another body with yet another set of rules.

Each of these is what we call an instance of the architecture. Different instances make different decisions — about when to recognize a transaction, how to quantify it, what to disclose. But no instance can break the architecture. The identity still holds. Double-entry still holds. The four windows remain.

In this prework, we study one instance in depth — US GAAP, applied to a real company, CVS Health. But the goal is not to memorize the rules of that one instance. The goal is to learn the architecture well enough that when you encounter a different instance — a different set of rules, a different kind of organization — it feels like a new facade on a familiar building, not like starting from scratch.

Whenever a rule appears that's specific to US GAAP, we'll flag it: this is an instance choice. It could have been made differently. It is made differently elsewhere. The architecture is what stays.

## THE ROADMAP

Let me show you where we're headed. The prework walks through the system from the outside in.

We start — right here, in Module 1 — with orientation. What kind of thing is this system, and how is this prework organized.

Module 2 asks: why does the system exist? Who uses it, and what questions do they bring? Investors, lenders, managers, regulators, courts — they all rely on the same system's outputs, but for very different reasons. Understanding those reasons is how you understand why the system evolved the way it did.

Module 3 turns to how the system processes what it takes in. How does it quantify the worth of things? What conventions does it use, and what tradeoffs do those conventions involve? And where does the system go silent — what kinds of economic reality does it simply not capture?

Module 4 is the engine — the accounting identity itself, and how it generates the four financial statements as four windows onto one structure.

Modules 5 through 7 put you inside one real instance. You'll work with CVS Health's actual financial statements — balance sheets, income statement, cash flows — and see the architecture and the instance choices operating together on real numbers.

Module 8 closes the loop. Having seen the system's outputs, you learn the input language — debits and credits, journal entries, T-accounts. How economic events actually enter the system.

By the end, you'll have walked the entire system: why it exists, how it works, what its outputs look like, and how inputs get in.

---

## HOW TO USE THIS PREWORK

One last thing. This prework is designed so you can move through it at your own pace.

If you have no accounting background, start at the beginning and take your time. Each module builds on the one before it.

If you've had some exposure to accounting, you'll find a diagnostic assessment before Module 4. It will help you figure out where you can move quickly and where to slow down. But even if the mechanics are familiar, don't skip the framing in Modules 1 through 3. The information system perspective — the architecture, the tradeoffs, the idea that every number is the product of design choices — that will be new for virtually everyone, including students who've taken intermediate accounting.

All right. Let's get started.
