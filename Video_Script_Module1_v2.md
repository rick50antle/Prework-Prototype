# Welcome and Orientation — Video Script (v2)

**Speaker:** Rick Antle
**Target length:** ~3 minutes
**Setting:** Direct to camera, conversational

---

Hi. I'm Rick Antle. I teach accounting at Yale, and I designed this prework for students going into their first accounting course — MBA, law, policy, anywhere you'll encounter financial information and want to understand what it's telling you and what it isn't.

No prior accounting knowledge required. If you do have some, you'll find we approach familiar territory from a different angle.

Here's where we're going to start. Think about a company like CVS Health — thousands of stores, millions of transactions a day, billions in debt, contracts that trigger legal consequences when certain numbers cross certain thresholds. No one can hold all of that in their head. No group of people can hold it without some shared system for recording, organizing, and communicating what happened.

That's why accounting systems exist. They exist because economic activity, past a certain scale, is too complex for anyone to track unaided. They take that complexity and compress it into something structured enough for a human mind to work with.

But here's the thing about compression: it requires choices. What do you include? What do you leave out? How do you quantify something whose worth might be contested? Every one of those choices involves a tradeoff. The output is useful — indispensable, even — but it is never the whole picture. It always requires a person who understands what the system did and what it didn't.

Climate models work the same way. Dashboards work the same way. Any system that takes complex reality and produces structured output for human use works the same way. Accounting systems belong to this family — and understanding that is the key to reading their output well.

Now, one distinction that will organize everything you're about to learn. Every accounting system on earth shares the same deep structure — an identity that forces everything to balance, a double-entry system, four financial statements that present different views of the same data. None of this was invented all at once. Double-entry emerged in medieval Italy. The structure crystallized over centuries, across cultures, because it worked.

We call that the architecture. What varies are the specific rules that different bodies write for different kinds of organizations. US GAAP for American corporations. IFRS for most of the rest of the world. Governmental rules for state and local governments. Each of those is an instance of the architecture.

In this prework, we study one instance — US GAAP, applied to CVS Health. But the goal is the architecture. Whenever a GAAP-specific rule appears, we'll flag it: this is an instance choice, and it could have been made differently. The architecture is what stays.

The prework walks through the system from the outside in. Why the system exists. How it processes what it takes in — the conventions, the tradeoffs, the limits. The engine at the center — the accounting identity and the four financial statements. Then one real company's financials, so you can see the architecture and the instance choices working together. And finally, the input language — debits, credits, journal entries.

By the end, you'll have walked the entire system.

If you've had some prior exposure, there's a diagnostic before Module 4 that will help you figure out where to go deep and where to move quickly. But don't skip Modules 1 through 3 — the framing will be new even if the mechanics aren't.

All right. Let's get started.
