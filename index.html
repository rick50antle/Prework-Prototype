Accounting as an Information System
Welcome · ~2 min
Beats 1 & 2 — Scale problem and complexity

“Imagine you are running a small business. Three employees, a handful of customers, maybe a dozen transactions in a week. You could probably keep track of most of that in your head — or a notebook. Now imagine ten thousand employees. Operations in forty states and a few different countries. Millions of transactions a year. Suppliers deciding whether to extend credit. Lenders deciding whether to lend. Shareholders deciding where to put their capital. Regulators checking for compliance. Each of them drawing on the same underlying records to answer very different questions. You can’t run that from a notebook. And the systems built to handle that complexity — to capture it, organize it, report it in a form people can actually use — those systems are genuinely remarkable. They’ve been evolving for literally thousands of years, absorbing new demands, new technology, new types of organizations. What we use today isn’t something a committee designed from scratch. It’s the accumulated solution to a very long series of real problems.”

Self-introduction

“My name is Rick Antle. I’m the William S. Beinecke Professor of Accounting at the Yale School of Management. I’ve spent my career studying how accounting systems work — what they reveal, what they conceal, and why. First encounters with accounting can be disorienting — whether you’ve worked through rules and procedures, or been dropped into a set of financial statements with no map to navigate them. What’s often missing is a real understanding of the architecture — where it came from, what problems it was built to solve, and why it looks the way it does. This prework is my attempt to fix that.”

One Architecture, Many Instances

Underneath every accounting system you’ll encounter — corporate annual reports, nonprofit statements, government budgets, your own household books — there is a common architecture. The same structural skeleton, customized along a small set of dimensions:

  • information desired
  • information available
  • type of organization
  • technology
  • context and setting

Those dimensions account for the visible variety. The architecture underneath them is the same. Once you can see it, the differences between a corporation, a charity, a municipal government, and a sole proprietorship are differences in how the same skeleton has been customized — not differences in what kind of object you are looking at.

This prework helps you build that architecture for yourself, from a problem rather than from rules. By the time formal labels arrive, you will have already constructed what they name. The architecture lands on something you own, not on something you have been told to memorize.

Module 1 builds the architecture from the ground up and ends with the $25 decision. Module 2 puts the architecture to use — what the records admit, what they leave out, and how the statements explain change over time. The final module reads the actual financial statements of CVS Health, one of the largest companies in the United States, so you can watch the architecture and US GAAP working together on real numbers.

A note for experienced students

If you’ve studied accounting before

You have likely already assembled a mental model of how the system works. The prework will ask you to set it aside while you are inside the activities — not because anything you previously learned was wrong, but because there is a real difference between applying a framework you already have and building one from scratch. The architecture has to land on what you have just done, not on what you already know.

Stay in the problems. The prior knowledge will be useful again later — and you may find that what you built here looks slightly different from what you remember, in ways that turn out to matter.
