Module 2 · Using the Identity

2.The Architecture: Introduction

You have an entity — an organization with a boundary. It has resources, and it has made promises. And it has a history — every resource and every promise reflects what the entity has done and what has happened to it. You have aspirations for what a financial story about that entity should capture: what resources it holds, what it owes, how those changed, and for whom. And you know that turning those aspirations into practice requires conventions, applied honestly within a context of incentives and oversight.

Now: how do you actually build the system?

Before any of that, you identified what counts as the entity — the work you did in the first activity, when you sorted what was personal from what was the business’s. With the entity fixed, the implementation rests on three structural moves.

Rabbit hole

What counts as the entity?

The entity boundary is a choice, not a fact about the world. Different boundaries produce different statements.

A small business and its owner. The business has its own bank account, inventory, and supplier debts. The owner has personal cash, a home, and a credit card balance. These are two different entities, even though the same person runs both. Sorting them apart — as you did in Activity 1 — is the first step in deciding what the accounting will and won’t capture.

A big company. CVS Pharmacy is a different entity from CVS Health. Pharmacy is one division of Health, and Health is the parent corporation that consolidates all the businesses underneath it. The 2018 acquisition of Aetna created another possible boundary: CVS Health on its own, or the CVS Health + Aetna consolidated group. Which one you account for changes the numbers.

A nonprofit. A university is one legal entity. Its endowment, its operating funds, and its donor-restricted funds are all inside that one corporation — but the university’s accounting presents them as separate “net asset classes” so users can see what the university can and can’t spend freely. The boundary at the corporate level is one choice; the boundary between fund types is another.

The general rule: the entity is whatever the accounting is of. Choosing the entity is the first step before anything else can happen. We’ll examine this more carefully later in Module 2.

  1. You built this You imposed a constraint — the accounting identity — that forces every recording to be internally consistent.
  2. Coming next You’ll apply that constraint at two different dates, creating two snapshots of the entity’s financial position.
  3. Coming next You’ll build channels that explain why the two snapshots differ — what happened between them.

Everything else in the system is elaboration on these three moves.
