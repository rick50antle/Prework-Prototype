# Welcome and Orientation — Video Script (v4)

**Speaker:** Rick Antle
**Target length:** ~3.5 minutes
**Setting:** Direct to camera, conversational

---

Hi. I'm Rick Antle. I teach accounting at Yale, and I designed this prework for students going into their first accounting course. That includes future MBAs, law students, policy students, future accountants and CPAs — anyone who will work with the information that accounting systems produce and wants to understand what that information is telling you and what it isn't.

No prior accounting knowledge required. If you do have some, you'll find we approach familiar territory from a different angle.

Here's where we start. Think about a company like CVS Health — thousands of stores, millions of transactions a day, billions in debt, contracts that trigger legal consequences when certain numbers cross certain thresholds. No one person can hold all of that in their head. But it's not just about any one person. Many different people want access to that same information — investors, lenders, managers, regulators, judges, tax authorities. They all want to understand what's happening in the same organization, and they want to coordinate their decisions and actions based on a shared picture of it.

That's why accounting systems exist. They are shared information systems — used by corporations, nonprofits, trusts, governments, tax systems — that help organizations and the people who depend on them cope with this complexity. Accounting systems compress that complexity into something structured enough for human minds to work with, and they make that structured picture available to everyone who relies on it.

But compression requires choices. There is far too much data to include it all, so every accounting system defines a boundary — what gets in and what doesn't. Beyond that boundary, you're choosing how to quantify things whose worth might be contested, when to recognize events, what to disclose. Every one of those choices involves a tradeoff. The output is useful — indispensable, even — but it is never the whole picture. It always requires a person who understands what the system did and what it didn't.

Now, one idea that will organize everything you're about to learn. Accounting systems around the world share a remarkable amount of deep structure. Most use double-entry bookkeeping — recording every economic event in two places. Most impose a self-balancing requirement — an identity — that keeps everything internally consistent. Most produce financial statements that present different views of the same underlying data. And all of them define that boundary — which economic events enter the system and which don't.

None of this emerged all at once. Double-entry bookkeeping appeared in medieval Italy. The balancing requirement crystallized through centuries of practice. These features survived and spread — across cultures, across centuries, into corporations and governments and tax systems alike — because they worked.

We call that shared deep structure the architecture. What varies are the specific rules that different bodies write for different kinds of organizations. US GAAP for American corporations. IFRS for most of the rest of the world. Separate rules for governments, for nonprofits, for tax reporting. Each of those is an instance of the architecture — a specific set of choices about when to recognize a transaction, how to quantify it, what to disclose.

In this prework, we take a high-altitude overview of that architecture. But it wouldn't be accounting if we didn't get into some specifics — so we'll study one instance, US GAAP, applied to a real company, CVS Health. Whenever a GAAP-specific rule appears, we'll flag it: this is an instance choice, and it could have been made differently. The architecture is what stays.

We'll work through the system from the outside in. Why it exists — who uses it and what questions they bring. How it processes what it takes in — the conventions, the tradeoffs, the limits. The engine at the center — the self-balancing requirement and the financial statements it generates. Then CVS Health's actual financials, so you can see the architecture and the instance choices working together on real numbers.

And finally, debits and credits. No introduction to accounting would be complete without them. If you aspire to be an accountant or a CPA, this is the language you use to provide inputs to the system. And if you're going to use financial statements in a sophisticated way — if you want to dig beneath the surface — the language of debits and credits is an analytical tool you'll reach for again and again.

One last thing. Keep in mind that this is the prework, not the course. Learning how a system that compresses this much complexity actually works — that's itself a complex task, one that takes real time and effort. Our goal here is to help you get into the right mindset, so that when the course begins, you're oriented and ready to acquire that understanding more efficiently. Think of this as coaching before the season starts.

If you've had some prior exposure to accounting, there's a diagnostic before Module 4 that will help you figure out where to go deep and where to move quickly. But don't skip Modules 1 through 3 — the framing will be new even if the mechanics aren't.

All right. Let's get started.
